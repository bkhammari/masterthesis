\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

This chapter presents the empirical findings in four stages. Section~\ref{sec:descriptive} establishes the geographic structure of treatment variation. Section~\ref{sec:pooled_results} reports the primary pooled event-study estimates for wages and employment. Section~\ref{sec:cohort_results} decomposes these effects using the Duflo-style age-cohort design. Section~\ref{sec:heterogeneity} explores distributional impacts by gender and ethnicity, and Section~\ref{sec:robustness_results} reports robustness checks.

\subsection{Geographic Distribution of Treatment Intensity}
\label{sec:descriptive}

Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} displays the spatial distribution of cumulative ProUni scholarship density ($D_{r,2019}$) across Brazil's 558 microregions. The map reveals a pronounced geographic concentration: the South and Southeast---particularly the S\~ao Paulo metropolitan corridor, Porto Alegre, and Belo Horizonte---exhibit the highest treatment intensities, while the Amazon basin and parts of the Northeast received substantially lower exposure. This pattern reflects the pre-existing private HEI infrastructure rather than any targeting based on local labor market outcomes, supporting the institutional exogeneity argument developed in Section~\ref{sec:assumptions}.

\subsection{Pooled Event-Study Estimates}
\label{sec:pooled_results}

\subsubsection{Wages: The Saturation Hypothesis}

Figure~\ref{fig:dose_response} presents the headline result. The dynamic event-study estimates reveal a clear divergence between Low-Dose and High-Dose microregions following treatment onset.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{Figures/fig_1_mechanism_dose.png}
\caption{\textbf{Dose-Response: High vs.\ Low Exposure Intensity.} Dynamic ATT estimates from \citeA{CallawaySantAnna2021}, aggregated via \texttt{aggte(type = ``dynamic'')}. Shaded regions denote 95\% pointwise confidence intervals. The dashed vertical line marks $e = -1$, the last pre-treatment period.}
\label{fig:dose_response}
\end{figure}

Three features of Figure~\ref{fig:dose_response} merit emphasis. First, pre-treatment coefficients ($e < 0$) for both dose bins fluctuate closely around zero, with no systematic upward or downward drift, supporting the conditional parallel trends assumption (A1). The joint Wald test fails to reject the null of zero pre-trends at conventional levels for both specifications ($p = 0.359$ for Low Dose; $p = 0.916$ for High Dose).

Second, the Low-Dose event study displays a gradual, monotonically increasing post-treatment trajectory, reaching an effect of approximately 2.5--3.0\% by $e = 5$--$6$. This cumulative pattern is consistent with the progressive entry of ProUni graduates into local labor markets. The overall simple ATT estimate (pooling all post-treatment cohort-time cells) for the Low-Dose bin is $1.28\%$ ($SE = 0.0128$). While the confidence intervals are wide---a consequence of dilution in the aggregate microregion-level data---the direction and magnitude are economically meaningful.

Third, the High-Dose estimates remain centered on zero throughout the post-treatment window. The overall simple ATT is precisely $-0.70\%$ ($SE = 0.0074$). The contrast between Low and High is precisely what the saturation hypothesis predicts: in microregions where the educational supply shock is moderate, scarcity rents are preserved; where the shock is massive, credential inflation and labor market congestion erode the returns.

\subsubsection{Employment: Extensive Margin}

Figure~\ref{fig:employment} reports the employment-margin estimates. Both dose bins show near-zero effects for the first four post-treatment years. The overall ATT on log formal employment is $1.82\%$ ($SE = 0.0332$) for Low Dose and $0.65\%$ ($SE = 0.0396$) for High Dose. 

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{Figures/fig_3_employment_dose.png}
\caption{\textbf{Employment Margin: High vs.\ Low Exposure.} ATT on log formal employment (v\'inculos, ages 22--35).}
\label{fig:employment}
\end{figure}

The absence of large employment effects is not surprising in a context where RAIS captures only formal employment. ProUni may shift workers from informal to formal jobs without changing the total headcount, an extensive-margin reallocation that the employment outcome cannot fully capture.

\subsection{Age-Cohort Decomposition}
\label{sec:cohort_results}

\subsubsection{Headline: Cohort Wage Gap}

To isolate the age-specific human capital channel, I estimate the treatment effect on the within-microregion wage gap between the exposed cohort (ages 22--28) and the control cohort (ages 29--35), as described in Section~\ref{sec:estimation_equations}. Figure~\ref{fig:cohort_gap} reports the results.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{Figures/fig_cohort_gap.png}
\caption{\textbf{Cohort Wage Gap: Young (22--28) vs.\ Old (29--35).} ATT on the within-microregion wage gap ($\Delta w_{r,t}$). A positive ATT indicates that ProUni raised the relative wage of the young (exposed) cohort.}
\label{fig:cohort_gap}
\end{figure}

The cohort wage gap results reveal a pattern opposite to the pooled estimates: the High-Dose series shows a modest positive drift after treatment (overall ATT of $2.34\%$, $SE = 0.0273$), while the Low-Dose series remains flat or turns slightly negative (overall ATT of $-0.54\%$, $SE = 0.0120$). The confidence intervals are substantially wider than in the pooled analysis, reflecting the additional noise introduced by the cohort differencing.

\subsubsection{Decomposition: Young-Only vs.\ Old-Only}

To understand what drives the cohort wage gap dynamics, I estimate the ATT separately for each age group. Figures~\ref{fig:young_old_low} and \ref{fig:young_old_high} report these decompositions.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{Figures/fig_cohort_young_vs_old.png}
\caption{\textbf{Age-Specific Effects: Low Dose.} Separate ATTs for young (22--28, exposed) and old (29--35, control cohort).}
\label{fig:young_old_low}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{Figures/fig_cohort_young_vs_old_high.png}
\caption{\textbf{Age-Specific Effects: High Dose.} Separate ATTs for young (22--28) and old (29--35).}
\label{fig:young_old_high}
\end{figure}

The decomposition reveals a nuanced pattern. In Low-Dose microregions (Figure~\ref{fig:young_old_low}), the young cohort shows a modest positive trajectory, but the old cohort---which should be unaffected by ProUni under strict partial equilibrium---displays an even stronger positive drift. This implies positive \emph{general equilibrium spillovers}: when a moderate number of college graduates enter a local labor market, the resulting productivity improvements benefit all workers.

In High-Dose microregions (Figure~\ref{fig:young_old_high}), the pattern reverses. The young cohort remains near zero, while the old cohort \emph{declines} markedly. This is consistent with a \emph{displacement} mechanism: when the supply of college-educated young workers is large enough to saturate local demand, older workers experience relative wage losses.

\subsubsection{First Stage: College Attainment Gap}

Figure~\ref{fig:first_stage} reports the estimated effect of ProUni density on the college share gap between young and old workers.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{Figures/fig_first_stage.png}
\caption{\textbf{First Stage: College Share Gap (Young $-$ Old).} ATT on the difference in college-educated worker shares between age cohorts.}
\label{fig:first_stage}
\end{figure}

The first-stage estimates are exceptionally small in magnitude for most post-treatment periods. For High-Dose microregions, the overall ATT is $0.24\%$ ($SE = 0.0031$), while for Low-Dose it is effectively zero. The weakness of the first stage reflects two features of the data: (a) RAIS records education level with severe measurement error, and (b) the college share denominator includes all formal workers, diluting the signal. As a consequence, computing a Wald LATE estimate (by dividing the reduced-form wage effect by the first stage) yields highly imprecise and implausibly large point estimates (e.g., $960\%$ with a standard error of $1656\%$ in the High Dose bin), reaffirming that these estimates should be interpreted purely as reduced-form policy parameters.

\subsection{Heterogeneity by Gender and Ethnicity}
\label{sec:heterogeneity}

\subsubsection{Pooled Heterogeneity}

\paragraph{Gender.}
Figure~\ref{fig:gender_pooled} reports the gender-stratified pooled event studies. In Low-Dose microregions, male workers exhibit a positive and growing wage effect (overall ATT $1.67\%$, $SE = 0.0169$), while female workers remain indistinguishable from zero throughout (overall ATT $-0.03\%$, $SE = 0.0007$). This asymmetry is striking given that women constitute the majority of ProUni beneficiaries. In High-Dose microregions, both genders show near-zero effects.

\subsection{Robustness Checks}
\label{sec:robustness_results}

\subsubsection{TWFE Benchmark}

Table~\ref{tab:twfe} compares the naive TWFE estimates with the \citeA{CallawaySantAnna2021} ATTs. The TWFE wage coefficient is $-0.65\%$ ($SE = 0.0037$), completely masking the positive effect of $1.28\%$ observed in the Low-Dose CS specification. The TWFE estimate represents a weighted average of the two dose bins contaminated by negative weights, confirming that pooling across heterogeneous treatment intensities and time periods severely distorts the evaluation.

\subsubsection{Unconditional vs.\ Doubly-Robust Estimation}

To ensure results are not driven by pre-existing regional differences, I apply the doubly-robust estimator conditioning on pre-treatment Census covariates. The Doubly-Robust estimate for Low-Dose yields a larger point estimate of $2.59\%$ ($SE = 0.0145$), confirming that the positive scarcity effect holds even after adjusting for baseline demographic and economic covariates.

The results support four main conclusions. First, ProUni generates an economically meaningful positive wage effect in Low-Dose microregions, consistent with the scarcity hypothesis. Second, High-Dose microregions show null or slightly negative effects on pooled wages, confirming the saturation hypothesis. Third, the age-cohort decomposition reveals that in Low-Dose areas, wage gains extend to older workers through general equilibrium spillovers, while in High-Dose areas, the inflow of graduates displaces older workers. Fourth, ProUni's racial quotas successfully direct benefits toward non-white workers, though the male wage advantage remains a persistent feature of the Brazilian labor market.
