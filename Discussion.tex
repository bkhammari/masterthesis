\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Interpreting the Pooled Effects: Magnitude and Mechanism}

The main reduced-form result of this thesis is a positive wage effect in Low-Dose microregions and an attenuated or null effect in High-Dose areas. This finding is economically interesting, but it must be interpreted with care. The estimates are measured on the average wage of \emph{all} formal workers aged 22--35 within a microregion, not on individual ProUni beneficiaries. Because beneficiaries constitute only a modest share of the local formal workforce, any aggregate effect is necessarily diluted relative to the private return earned by marginal graduates.

That dilution is one reason why statistical precision is limited. The thesis should therefore not be read as pinning down a precise individual return to a ProUni degree. Instead, the contribution is to show that the local reduced-form effect of educational expansion appears more positive in moderately exposed markets than in heavily exposed ones. This is a meaningful policy result even when confidence intervals remain wide, because the estimand is intentionally demanding: it asks whether a targeted higher-education programme can shift average local formal-sector outcomes.

\subsection{Scarcity versus Saturation: Evidence and Caution}

The contrast between Low-Dose and High-Dose microregions is consistent with a scarcity-versus-attenuation interpretation. In markets that receive a moderate inflow of graduates, wage gains appear more positive; in heavily exposed markets, the estimates move closer to zero. That pattern fits economic theories of diminishing marginal returns to educational expansion and potential congestion in local skilled labour markets.

At the same time, the thesis cannot claim to have fully identified a causal dose-response curve. The methods chapter makes clear that standard parallel trends identifies within-stratum effects, whereas clean comparisons across dose strata require stronger assumptions. Moreover, the continuous-treatment TWFE robustness exercise shows that the negative high-dose pattern is sensitive to controls for baseline income and macroeconomic geography. For that reason, the safest formulation is that the data are \emph{consistent with} saturation or attenuation in heavily exposed markets, not that they prove it conclusively.

The age-cohort decomposition adds suggestive evidence on mechanisms. In Low-Dose areas, the rise in older workers' wages is consistent with positive spillovers, such as improved firm-worker matching or broader productivity gains. In High-Dose areas, weaker young-cohort gains and falling older-cohort wages are consistent with relative compression or displacement. Yet those patterns do not uniquely identify a single structural mechanism. Since the older cohort also functions as a placebo group, non-zero effects on that cohort remain a warning that residual local shocks may still be present.

\subsection{Heterogeneity: Gender and Ethnicity}

The gender results suggest that educational expansion is not translated into formal-sector wage gains symmetrically across workers. In the pooled estimates, men benefit more clearly than women in Low-Dose microregions even though women form a large share of ProUni beneficiaries. This asymmetry is consistent with several mechanisms already emphasized in the Brazilian labour-economics literature: field-of-study sorting, occupational segregation, unequal access to high-return jobs, and different labour-force attachment.

The ethnicity results are more encouraging, but they also require disciplined phrasing. In moderately exposed markets, the estimates are consistent with positive wage effects for non-white workers and with a narrowing of relative disadvantage within the formal sector. That is an important finding given ProUni's affirmative-action design. Still, the thesis does not show that broader ethnoracial inequality has disappeared, nor can it claim full parity in labour-market opportunities on the basis of reduced-form RAIS estimates alone.

\subsection{Geographic Concentration and the Bel\'india Problem}

The geography of ProUni exposure reveals a core policy tension. Scholarships are most concentrated where the private higher-education sector is already thickest, namely in the South and Southeast. Those regions also experienced the deepest interaction between high programme exposure and adverse macroeconomic conditions during the sample period. As a result, the programme's realised spatial incidence does not align neatly with the places where local returns may be highest.

This mismatch is suggestive rather than dispositive, but it matters for policy design. If marginal returns to educational expansion are indeed larger in under-served labour markets, then a policy architecture that depends heavily on existing private HEI capacity may underprovide scholarships precisely where local scarcity is greatest. A more balanced formulation than a strong policy prescription is therefore that the current evidence motivates closer evaluation of regional targeting and supply constraints in Brazil's private higher-education system.

\subsection{Limitations and Statistical Precision}
\label{sec:limitations}

Several limitations qualify the interpretation of the results. First, the analysis is restricted to the \emph{formal} labour market. RAIS does not observe informal employment, self-employment, or non-employment, so the thesis estimates effects on formal-sector outcomes rather than on total labour-market welfare. If ProUni mainly improves access to formal jobs, the aggregate wage results may understate an important formalisation channel while also mixing wage and composition effects.

Second, the treatment variable measures scholarship \emph{grants}, not observed degree \emph{completions}. Dropout and non-completion in private higher education create classical measurement error that attenuates treatment intensity and weakens any first-stage link to tertiary attainment. This limitation is visible in the very small college-share effects estimated from RAIS.

Third, the first-stage weakness means the thesis cannot credibly recover a precise Wald-style return to a ProUni degree from these aggregate data. The unstable LATE calculations are best understood as evidence that the regional reduced-form design is informative about policy effects but poorly suited to recovering individual causal returns through a noisy education measure.

Finally, the thesis cannot fully separate ProUni from the broader higher-education policy environment, especially the expansion of FIES and the federal quota law. Time fixed effects absorb common national shocks, but spatially heterogeneous interactions between these policies and local higher-education infrastructure may still contaminate the estimates. The results should therefore be interpreted as evidence on ProUni's contribution within a broader policy mix rather than as an isolated partial-equilibrium parameter.
