\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:intro}

Since its inception in 2005, Brazil's \emph{Programa Universidade para Todos} (ProUni) has awarded over three million scholarships to students from low-income families, making it one of the largest higher-education access programs in the developing world. By offering full and partial tuition waivers at private universities in exchange for tax exemptions, ProUni dramatically expanded college enrollment among populations historically excluded from tertiary education---particularly non-white students, women, and residents of underserved regions. Yet despite its scale and political salience, rigorous causal evidence on whether ProUni translates into better labor market outcomes remains surprisingly scarce.

This thesis asks a simple but consequential question: \emph{Does ProUni raise the wages of its beneficiaries, and if so, by how much?} Answering this question is complicated by the absence of individual-level linked data connecting scholarship recipients to their subsequent labor market trajectories. I therefore adopt an ecological approach, exploiting geographic and temporal variation in ProUni scholarship density across Brazil's 558 microregions to identify the program's causal effect on formal-sector wages.

The central methodological contribution of this thesis is the application of a Duflo-style age-cohort comparison \shortciteA{Duflo2001} to the ProUni context. Rather than estimating a single average treatment effect across all working-age adults, I decompose the workforce into an \emph{exposed cohort} (ages 22--28, who could plausibly be ProUni graduates) and a \emph{control cohort} (ages 29--35, too old to have benefited from ProUni at standard university-going age). The within-microregion wage gap between these two cohorts nets out any local economic shocks that affect all ages equally, isolating the age-specific human capital channel. Combined with the spatial variation in ProUni intensity, this yields a triple-difference (DDD) design estimated using the heterogeneity-robust estimator of \citeA{CallawayAndSantAnna2021}.

The main findings are as follows. First, ProUni significantly increases the relative wage of the exposed (young) cohort in low-dose microregions, with an implied Wald/LATE estimate of the return to a ProUni degree of [FILL]\%. Second, this effect exhibits clear saturation: high-dose microregions show attenuated returns, consistent with the diminishing returns to education expansion documented by \citeA{Duflo2001}. Third, the old-cohort placebo confirms that the treatment effect is age-specific rather than reflecting general local prosperity. Fourth, heterogeneity analysis reveals [FILL: gender and race patterns].

The remainder of this thesis is organized as follows. Chapter~\ref{ch:litreview} reviews the literature on higher-education subsidies and their labor market effects, with particular attention to the Brazilian context. Chapter~\ref{ch:background} provides institutional background on ProUni and the Brazilian labor market. Chapter~\ref{ch:experiment} details the identification strategy and econometric framework. Chapter~\ref{ch:results} presents the main results, robustness checks, and heterogeneity analysis. Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion} interprets the findings and discusses policy implications. Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion} concludes.
