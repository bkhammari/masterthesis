\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This thesis evaluates the labor market consequences of Brazil's \acrlong{prouni} program using a heterogeneity-robust staggered difference-in-differences design applied to administrative employer--employee data covering 558 microregions over the period 2005--2019. By discretizing the continuous scholarship density into dose bins and applying the \citeA{CallawaySantAnna2021} estimator, the analysis exploits both the spatial intensity and staggered temporal adoption of the program to identify its causal effects on formal wages and employment.

Three principal findings emerge. First, ProUni generates an economically meaningful, albeit statistically imprecise, positive wage effect of approximately 1.28--2.59\% in Low-Dose microregions, where the educational supply shock is moderate relative to local labor demand. In High-Dose microregions---concentrated in the South and Southeast---the aggregate effect is indistinguishable from zero ($-0.70\%$), consistent with a saturation mechanism in which the wage premium is eroded by credential inflation. This finding extends the seminal results of \citeA{Duflo2001} to a contemporary higher education context: diminishing marginal returns to educational expansion are not limited to primary schooling in developing countries but apply equally to tertiary education in a large middle-income economy.

Second, the Duflo-style age-cohort decomposition reveals that in Low-Dose areas, the positive wage effects extend to older workers (ages 29--35) who could not have been direct ProUni beneficiaries, indicating positive general equilibrium spillovers that raise local productivity beyond the directly treated cohort. In High-Dose areas, the mechanism reverses: older workers experience wage losses consistent with competitive displacement by the influx of young graduates. The social return to education thus depends critically on the scale of the intervention.

Third, ProUni's racial quota provisions achieve parity in wage returns between white and non-white workers in moderate-intensity markets, suggesting that the program effectively bridges the structural barriers documented in the \emph{Bel\'india} literature. However, the male wage advantage in the pooled analysis---despite women constituting the majority of beneficiaries---points to persistent gender segmentation in the conversion of educational credentials into labor market returns.

These findings have direct policy implications for the design of higher education expansion in developing countries. The geographic mismatch between ProUni's treatment intensity (concentrated in the wealthy Southeast) and the regions where returns are highest (the underserved North and Northeast) suggests that redistributing scholarships toward lower-density markets would improve the program's cost-effectiveness. More broadly, the saturation evidence cautions against the assumption that scaling up educational access will produce proportional improvements in labor market outcomes: the returns to education are endogenous to the scale of the intervention.

Future research should address three limitations of the present study. First, individual-level panel data linking ProUni scholarship records to RAIS employment histories would enable direct estimation of individual returns, bypassing the aggregation problem that dilutes the current estimates. Second, incorporating informal labor market data (e.g., from the PNAD Cont\'inua) would capture formalization effects that RAIS alone cannot detect. Third, the interaction between ProUni, FIES, and the Lei de Cotas deserves a dedicated triple-difference design exploiting the differential timing of these policies to parse their distinct contributions to Brazil's higher education transformation.
