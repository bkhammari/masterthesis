\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This thesis studies the labour-market consequences of Brazil's \acrlong{prouni} using a heterogeneity-robust staggered difference-in-differences design applied to administrative employer--employee data for 558 microregions over the period 2005--2019. By combining variation in treatment timing with variation in local scholarship intensity, the analysis estimates reduced-form effects of ProUni exposure on formal wages and employment. The central empirical contribution is therefore not a precise estimate of the individual return to a scholarship, but evidence on whether local labour markets respond differently to moderate and heavy programme exposure.

Four main conclusions emerge. First, within Low-Dose microregions, the estimated effect on formal-sector wages is positive and economically meaningful, although statistically imprecise. In the baseline specification, the overall ATT is around 1.28\%, and it rises to about 2.59\% in the Doubly Robust specification. Second, estimated wage effects are weaker in High-Dose microregions and often close to zero. This pattern is consistent with attenuation of gains in heavily exposed markets, but the thesis does not claim to have cleanly identified a causal dose-response curve: comparisons across dose strata require stronger assumptions, and the high-dose penalty becomes fragile once macroeconomic geography is controlled for.

Third, the age-cohort decomposition provides suggestive evidence on mechanisms rather than definitive proof. In moderately exposed markets, the results are consistent with positive spillovers that may extend beyond directly exposed younger workers. In heavily exposed markets, they are consistent with relative wage compression or displacement affecting older workers. At the same time, the old cohort also serves as a placebo benchmark, so non-zero effects on that group caution against overly strong structural interpretation.

Fourth, the heterogeneity analysis suggests that ProUni's gains are not distributed uniformly. In Low-Dose markets, the wage effects are more favourable for non-white workers than for white workers, a pattern consistent with the programme's affirmative-action design. By contrast, men appear to benefit more clearly than women in the pooled formal-sector estimates. These results point to persistent differences in how educational expansion is translated into labour-market returns across groups.

Taken together, the findings support a disciplined interpretation of ProUni as a policy that can improve local formal-sector wage outcomes, but whose returns depend on market context and cannot be assumed to scale proportionally with programme intensity. The evidence is strongest for positive reduced-form effects in moderately exposed labour markets. It is much weaker for strong claims about saturation, displacement, or exact individual returns.

These results also carry measured policy implications. Because ProUni relies on existing private higher-education infrastructure, the spatial distribution of scholarships is concentrated in the South and Southeast. If marginal returns to expansion are indeed larger where skilled labour remains relatively scarce, then the current institutional architecture may not align exposure with the locations where social returns are highest. The thesis does not establish that conclusion definitively, but it does motivate closer attention to regional targeting, local absorptive capacity, and the interaction between higher-education policy and regional labour-market structure.

Several limitations remain. First, the analysis is restricted to the formal labour market observed in RAIS and therefore cannot capture informal employment, self-employment, or non-employment. Second, treatment is measured using scholarship grants rather than observed degree completion, which introduces attenuation through incomplete take-up and dropout. Third, the weak first stage for tertiary attainment in RAIS prevents credible recovery of a precise Wald-style return to a ProUni degree from these aggregate data. Finally, the estimates should be interpreted in the context of Brazil's broader higher-education policy mix, including FIES and the federal quota law, whose spatial interactions cannot be fully separated here.

Future research could build on this thesis in three directions. Linking individual ProUni scholarship records to RAIS histories would allow direct estimation of beneficiary-level returns and substantially reduce the aggregation problem. Integrating informal-sector information from household surveys would clarify whether ProUni mainly raises wages, formalizes workers, or both. More broadly, a design that jointly studies ProUni, FIES, and quota policies would help disentangle how different channels of higher-education expansion interacted during Brazil's educational transformation.
